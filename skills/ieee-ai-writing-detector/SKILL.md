## STEP 1: Identify AI-like and Weak Sentences

Read the entire text carefully. Flag sentences that exhibit one or more of these patterns:

### Pattern A — Generic / Content-Free
Sentences that could appear in any paper on any topic. They sound professional but contain no information specific to this research.
- "This approach offers significant advantages over existing methods."
- "The proposed framework is highly effective and efficient."
- "This work makes several important contributions to the field."

### Pattern B — Vague Overclaim
Strong-sounding claims with no evidence, specificity, or qualification. Often use absolute or superlative language.
- "Our method outperforms all existing approaches."
- "This is the first work to address this problem."
- "The results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method."

### Pattern C — Rhythmic Repetition / Template Structure
Multiple consecutive sentences with the same syntactic structure, often generated by cycling through a template.
- "First, we propose X. Second, we implement Y. Third, we evaluate Z." (three identical structures in a row)
- Opening every paragraph with "In this section, we..."
- Repeating "Furthermore," / "Moreover," / "In addition," as paragraph connectors throughout

### Pattern D — Hollow Hedging
Sentences that qualify every claim to the point of saying nothing, or use passive constructions to avoid commitment.
- "It can be seen that the results suggest that the approach may potentially improve performance."
- "The method appears to offer some advantages in certain scenarios."

### Pattern E — Missing Specificity
Claims that require concrete numbers, names, or mechanisms but provide none.
- "The model was trained on a large dataset." (How large? What dataset?)
- "Performance improved significantly." (By how much? On what metric?)
- "We used a state-of-the-art language model." (Which one?)

### Pattern F — Unnecessary Throat-Clearing
Introductory filler phrases that add no content and delay the actual point.
- "It is worth noting that..."
- "It is important to mention that..."
- "As can be observed from the results..."
- "In order to better understand..."

## STEP 2: Rewrite Each Flagged Sentence

For each flagged sentence, provide:
1. The original sentence (quoted exactly)
2. The specific pattern(s) it matches and why it is problematic
3. An improved version that is specific, grounded, and natural

The improved version must:
- Retain the original meaning and scope
- Replace vague language with specific language (use `[X]` placeholders only when data is missing and cannot be inferred)
- Vary sentence structure from surrounding sentences
- Sound like it was written by a researcher who knows their own work in detail
- NOT introduce new claims or facts not present in the original

---

## STEP 3: Overall Style Assessment

After reviewing individual sentences, assess the text as a whole:
- Overall writing authenticity and naturalness
- Structural patterns that repeat across the section
- Tone appropriateness for IEEE academic writing
- Whether the text sounds like expert writing or generated text

IMPORTANT REMINDERS:
- Do NOT flag every sentence — only flag sentences with real, identifiable problems. Flagging good sentences reduces trust in the review.
- The goal of an improved sentence is not to make it sound more formal — it is to make it more specific and grounded.
- Do not invent numbers, results, or technical details in the rewrites — use `[specify: X]` placeholders for missing data.
- Pattern F (throat-clearing filler) is common and easy to fix — always flag it when present.
- A sentence is not AI-like just because it is grammatically correct — it must match one of the defined patterns above.
- Short, direct, evidence-backed sentences are the target — not longer, more elaborate ones.
